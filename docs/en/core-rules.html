Core Rules

Game Master and Player

Players enter an adventure: a story created by the Game Master. They take the roles of fictional Characters and describe their actions. The Game Master describes the consequences.

Ranks

Many parts of the system use ranks. Ranks make character capabilities easier to understand and reduce the amount of numbers and math.

Ranks should be used wherever progress, quality, training, or mastery needs to be shown. For example, a scholar who has never held a weapon might have rapier skill at D or E, but alchemy at A or B.

The same idea can be used for tools, weapons, and items to describe their quality and capabilities.

Character

Players and the Game Master describe the actions, feelings, and thoughts of the Characters they play. A Character has Characteristics, Traits, and Abilities. A Player plays their own Character, while the Game Master plays all non-player Characters.

Characteristics

Each Character has characteristics that describe their abilities in different areas.

  1. Body
    Physical strength, endurance, pain tolerance, fatigue, and health.
    Example: strike force, body toughness, physical exercise.
  2. Agility
    Body control, precision, reaction, and coordination.
    Example: precise physical action, speed, balance, stealth.
  3. Mind
    Analysis, logic, memory, and the ability to connect facts.
    Example: investigation, analysis.
  4. Intuition
    Instinct, empathy, and the feeling that something is wrong.
    Example: gut feelings, inner instincts.
  5. Will
    Mental stability, self-control, and resistance to fear or pressure.
    Example: staying sane, resisting terrifying scenes, fear, or pressure.
  6. Influence
    Ability to affect others: charisma, authority, pressure.
    Example: charm, speech, intimidation, confidence, status.

Each characteristic has a rank and a numerical modifier:

  • S -> +6
  • A -> +4
  • B -> +2
  • C -> 0
  • D -> -2
  • E -> -4

For especially strong or weak cases, extreme ranks can be repeated, such as SSS or EEE.

Character Rank

The Character Rank is a general rank based on the character's characteristics. It is a loose summary of the character's overall power and does not directly affect rules.

For example, if a Character has characteristics A, B, B, C, C, E, they can be treated as a rank B character. These calculations can be interpreted freely and depend on the Game Master.

Stats

Health Points (HP)

Health Points are a numerical description of a Character's vitality. One simple way to calculate HP is to tie it to Body rank and the Character's Health Die. The Health Die can vary between characters, creatures, or archetypes. By default, use d10; a fragile mage might use d6, while a huge monster might use d12 or higher.

Basic example for d10:

Body RankHP
E10 + 2d10
D25 + 3d10
C45 + 4d10
B70 + 5d10
A100 + 6d10
S130 + 7d10

For example, a Character with Body B and a standard Health Die d10 gets 70 + 5d10 HP. If the same character is unusually fragile and has a Health Die d6, they get 70 + 5d6 HP.

This is only a basic example. If another health system fits your setting better, adapt it: fixed values, wound tracks, separate sanity, armor, or stamina tracks. The important part is that players understand what condition their Character is in.

Defense Rating (DR)

Defense Rating describes how physically protected a Character is. It can represent body toughness, dodging, fencing skill, or similar factors.

By default:

``text DR = 10 + Body modifier + Agility modifier ``

Armor and Effects can add extra modifiers.

This is a passive defense. The Game Master is also encouraged to use active defense based on the situation. For example, a normal body strike against a fencer can use passive DR, but an attempt to hit the eye should have higher DR, because the target will instinctively protect it.

Movement Speed

Movement speed shows how far a Character can move during one Turn.

By default, all Characters have a speed of 5 meters, but this can change based on the Character and situation.

Exhaustion

Exhaustion measures how overloaded a Character is. The Game Master can use it as a universal cost for abilities and difficult actions. It encourages players to act efficiently and creates tense situations where a character is alive but barely able to act.

Exhaustion is tracked numerically. When it crosses certain thresholds, the Character gains penalties. The Game Master can allow fixed exhaustion costs or allow players to spend more or less effort. If a player tries to spend a huge amount of exhaustion to win immediately, there should be an additional cost beyond simply crossing a threshold. Treat it as pushing the body beyond its limits.

Default exhaustion states:

  1. Character is full of strength and energy.
  2. Disadvantage on Checks.
  3. Cannot move without help and loses Bonus Action.
  4. Character falls unconscious.

The threshold can vary by game, but this default formula can be used:

``text ExhaustionThreshold_i = (5 + max(Body modifier, Mind modifier, Will modifier)) * i ``

Death

When a Character's HP reaches 0, they enter the "dying" state. They are completely unable to act. If nobody helps them, they die. In Combat, help must arrive within 3 turns, and every attack against the dying Character reduces the remaining number of turns. Outside combat, the time limit is 1 minute, and any attack immediately ends the chance to help. Helping a dying character is an Action.

Traits

Traits are features that define a Character's strengths and weaknesses. They should describe the character from different angles, add depth, and encourage roleplay. Traits should come from the character's story and personality.

Example traits for a soldier:

  • Discipline
    Years of strict training and mental toughness. +2 to fear checks.
  • Combat weapon training, rank A
  • Subordination
    Cannot easily disobey superiors. Will check with disadvantage to ignore orders.
  • Callousness
    A harsh childhood and military life reduced their empathy. Disadvantage on charm, sympathy, and similar checks.

Abilities

Abilities are special active actions a Character can use to strongly change a situation. They should have limits and costs so players do not abuse them. The cost should match the ability's power and the character's mastery of it.

For example, if a Character can fight with a halberd, normal attacks can be free. But if they invent an unusual technique, using it might cost exhaustion, and the ability rank might be low until the character masters it. Simple and common techniques may have no cost.

Effects

Effects are statuses applied to a Character. They can be positive, negative, or neutral.

For example, if a rogue secretly puts a laxative into a Character's tea, that character might gain the effect "stuck in the bathroom".

Shells

Optional mechanic.

Many settings have some form of "shell": full armor, temporary health, a magic shield, or similar protection. These can be described as shells - extra health layers that must be broken before the character takes damage directly.

Shells can have types. In a magic setting, a shell might block only magical or only physical damage. In a psychological setting, it might be a mental shell that protects sanity.

Inventory

Inventory is the set of Items a Character has available. The Game Master should allow players to carry items as long as they can explain how they store and move them. Weight can be tracked if needed, but most of the time common sense is enough.

Items and Weapons

Items and Weapons can also have ranks. A rank can describe quality, power, rarity, convenience, or how well the Character has mastered the item.

Examples:

  • a rusty saber might be rank D and give disadvantage on difficult fencing actions
  • a good military rapier might be rank B and work reliably without penalties
  • a legendary blade might be rank A or S, add bonuses, pierce special Shells, or unlock a separate Ability

An item rank does not always need to become a direct bonus. Sometimes it simply defines what the item can do. A normal knife should not easily pierce heavy armor even with a good roll, while an artifact hammer might break stone without a special formula.

Inspiration

Inspiration is a resource the Game Master gives to a Player, usually for good play or roleplay, but ultimately at the GM's discretion.

Inspiration can be spent to:

  • reroll any die
  • use a Character Ability outside its normal rules
  • give it to another Character for immediate use, but not for later

Optionally, the Game Master can use Anti-Inspiration. It is given for bad roleplay or acting against the character. Do not overuse it. The Game Master can spend it against the player who received it to:

  • reroll any die
  • cancel a Character Ability

Conflict

A Conflict is a situation whose outcome depends on Checks from several participants. The highest result wins. On a tie, the defending side wins.

For one-sided checks, the Game Master sets a passive Difficulty before the roll.

Difficulty

The Game Master sets the Difficulty based on the situation right before the check. It is a number. The easier and more convenient the action is, the lower the difficulty.

In opposed conflicts, characteristics usually clash directly, but the Game Master can add situational modifiers. Such a modifier should not exceed the maximum characteristic modifier in absolute value.

Check

Unless stated otherwise, a Check uses d20 plus the relevant characteristic modifier and any additional modifiers.

``text 1d20 + Characteristic modifier + Extra modifiers >= Difficulty ``

Critical Success and Failure

A critical success makes a Check successful regardless of modifiers and difficulty. A critical failure does the opposite. By default, a natural 20 is a critical success and a natural 1 is a critical failure. Traits and Abilities can change these values.

Critical success or failure can directly change the result to make the moment stand out, but it should not allow absurd outcomes.

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantage means the Character rolls twice and takes the better result. Disadvantage means they take the worse result.

A critical success overrides any disadvantage. A critical failure overrides any advantage.

Advantages and disadvantages usually should not stack. Pick the overall state and limit the roll to 2 dice unless a specific Trait or Ability says otherwise.

Combat

Combat is split into Rounds, and each round contains Turns for participating Characters.

Initiative

Before Combat, all participants roll initiative using Agility. This determines turn order. On a tie, tied characters reroll between themselves, or act in an order agreed by the players or decided by the Game Master.

Turns

Combat is divided into Turns, each belonging to one Character. Characters can delay their turn to the end of the round. Allied Characters whose turns are consecutive can act together.

Each Turn lasts roughly 6 seconds in the game world.

If players think for too long, the GM can push them narratively or use a real time limit. A good option is "hesitation": if a player cannot decide for too long, the character hesitates and loses the turn. This should also apply to the Game Master.

Zones

Combat space is divided into zones. A Character can move freely within their current zone. Moving beyond it costs movement.

Usually, a field can be divided into 1-meter cells and characters have 5 meters of movement, meaning 5 cells per turn. This is only an example. Movement systems can change with the setting and scene.

Actions

By default, a Character can take 1 Action and 1 Bonus Action per turn, not counting movement. Traits and Abilities can change this.

Actions must fit within the turn and be possible in the situation. Example actions:

  1. Attack
    Damage, push, wound, or otherwise harm an enemy.
  2. Study / Analyze
    Gain useful information about space, characters, items, and so on.
  3. Influence
    Affect another Character's behavior without a physical attack.
  4. Search
    Find hidden things.
  5. Resist
    Try to remove an Effect or resist its influence.
  6. Prepare
    Prepare a larger action for the next turn. This can also grant Advantage, such as aiming.
  7. Team Action
    Combine your Turn with another Character's Turn. Enemy turns must not separate the two turns.
  8. Interact
    Any action not listed above.

If an action can be done in a brief moment, it can be done as a Bonus Action.

Reactions

On another character's turn, a Character can only make Reactions: defend against area attacks, resist Effects, or respond to interaction with them or their immediate surroundings.

A Reaction must fit the same limits as a Bonus Action. By default, a Character can make 1 Reaction per Round. The Game Master decides what can be done as a reaction, while players can argue why a reaction should be possible.

Rest

During a game or campaign, Players need rests. Short Rests are usually started by players, while Long Rests usually happen between sessions.

Short Rest

During a Short Rest, Resources recover partially depending on the time spent resting, up to half of their maximum values.

Long Rest

During a Long Rest, all Resources recover, and all Effects and Exhaustion are cleared unless stated otherwise. Long rests usually happen automatically between sessions.
