For Players

Character Creation

A player starts by creating their Character. They should decide who they want to be in the adventure and what they can do. This should be discussed with the Game Master so the character fits the game.

Concept

Start with the character's concept: their rough goals, wishes, and capabilities. They should fit the setting provided by the Game Master.

Backstory

After the concept is approved, write the character's backstory so their idea has narrative grounding. The level of detail depends on your table. You can write only a sketch and let the Game Master fill gaps, or write more yourself and let the GM adjust it.

Mechanics

Based on the concept and the game systems, the player and GM define the character's mechanics:

  • Characteristics
  • Traits
  • Abilities

Equipment

Most characters do not appear empty-handed. Decide what inventory the character has at the start. As with backstory, this can be handled by the player, the Game Master, or both.

Progression

Character progression happens in several ways:

  • Changing Characteristics
    Characters can improve in some areas over time. If they neglect an area, characteristics can also decrease.
  • Increasing survivability
    After serious trials, the Game Master can reward Characters with more HP, a better Health Die, a defensive Trait, or a stronger Shell.
  • Traits and Abilities
    During adventures, Characters can change, transform, learn new things, gain new traits and abilities, or increase their ranks.
  • New Items
    New Items are also progression. The Game Master should give players chances to obtain useful items, unique artifacts, and sometimes even legendary things.

Roleplay

Roleplay is one of the most important parts of the game. Even combat-heavy games benefit from it. It adds drama, depth, immersion, and fun. Any interaction, conflict, or short exchange can become more engaging when roleplayed.

Simple ways to make roleplay easier:

  • Think about the character's voice and how it changes in different situations.
  • Think about facial expressions and reactions to sadness, joy, irritation, and so on.
  • Feel the character's story. If it is short, add small details and imagine more.
