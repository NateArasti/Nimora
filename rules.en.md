# Overview

The system focuses on creativity and imagination, so all mechanics are worded flexibly enough to make them easier to adapt to different worlds and game systems.

# Important Note

Most systems here are only a foundation and allow a very broad range of interpretation. This approach was chosen to make a wide range of games possible on top of simple core systems. It should let **Players** immerse themselves easily and **Game Masters** adapt the rules easily.

---

# What Do You Need To Play?

1. **Game Master**
   The person who creates the world, story, and adventures, manages the process, and runs the game.

2. **Players**
   People who take part in the game and play their **Characters**. The game follows their point of view, and the **Game Master** leads them through the adventure.

3. **Dice**
   You can use your own check systems or online dice rollers, but physical dice are strongly recommended.

4. ***Optional tools for immersion***:
   1. **Step away from the outside world**
      The game works better if everyone focuses on it for a few hours. Do not overdo this, but do not ignore focus either.
   2. **Table and chairs**
      It is much easier to stay focused when everyone sits together at one table instead of lying around on separate couches and chairs.
   3. **Game Master separation**
      This will probably happen naturally because the **Game Master** needs to keep watching the players, but it is still worth keeping some separation. The GM can sit apart, use a GM screen, a laptop, or something similar.
   4. **Character sheets**
      Physical or printed **Character** sheets help immersion. A character image also helps players picture and connect with their character. Players can write on these sheets, update their characters, and keep useful notes.
   5. **Props**
      Miniatures, battle maps, and similar things can help players immerse themselves and understand scenes, especially combat. They do not need to be expensive or detailed: dice or small household objects can stand in for miniatures, and a map can be drawn by hand. The rest comes from the GM's storytelling and the players' imagination.

# Core Rules

## Game Master and Player

**Players** enter an adventure: a story created by the **Game Master**. They take the roles of fictional **Characters** and describe their actions. The **Game Master** describes the consequences.

## Ranks

Many parts of the system use ranks. Ranks make character capabilities easier to understand and reduce the amount of numbers and math.
Ranks are strongly recommended wherever you need to show strength, rarity, importance, and similar qualities. In essence, they simplify level systems from other games.

Ranks:

- **`F`**
  Absolute nothing. As an item, it is literally useless trash or dust. As a **Body** characteristic, it could describe a mosquito.

- **`E`**
  An average person without any notable talents or skills. A sword of this rank would be rusty and still usable, but not very suitable for real combat.

- **`D`**
  An item of this quality is common, but at least worth some money. Someone who has only just learned to read in a typical fantasy-medieval setting would probably have this **Mind** rank.

- **`C`**
  This rank represents solid, dependable things. Town garrison knights or typical mercenaries probably wield a sword at about this level. Hacking cybersecurity at this rank in a cyberpunk world takes much more than pressing a couple of buttons on a phone.

- **`B`**
  People of this rank can already solve many problems on their own. "Excellent" is a good word for this rank. Newly published books and a standard sword made by a master smith belong here.

- **`A`**
  A truly superior rank. To reach it, you literally have to stand head and shoulders above everyone else.

- **`S`**
  This rank is extremely vague: at this level, comparisons become difficult because its subjects are *legendary*.
  Literally, the ***"Legendary Rank"***.

For especially strong or weak cases, extreme ranks can be repeated, such as SSS or FFF.

Ranks describe a position relative to the world's norms. In a distant cyberpunk setting, for example, simple literacy will hardly guarantee a **Character** a `D` rank in **Mind**.

For example, a scholar who has never held a weapon might have an `E` rank with a rapier but `A` or `B` alchemy skills. The same principle can be used for tools, weapons, and items to describe their quality and capabilities.

## Character

**Players** and the **Game Master** describe the actions, feelings, and thoughts of the **Characters** they play. A **Character** has **Characteristics**, **Traits**, and **Abilities**. A **Player** plays their own **Character**, while the **Game Master** plays all non-player **Characters**.

### Characteristics

Each **Character** has characteristics that describe their abilities and mastery in different areas, and each characteristic is described by a **Rank**.

1. **Body**
   Physical strength, endurance, pain tolerance, fatigue, and health.
   > Example: strike force, body toughness, physical exercise.

2. **Agility**
   Body control, precision, reaction, and coordination.
   > Example: precise physical action, speed, balance, stealth.

3. **Mind**
   Analysis, logic, memory, and the ability to connect facts.
   > Example: investigation, analysis.

4. **Intuition**
   Instinct, empathy, and the feeling that something is wrong.
   > Example: gut feelings, inner instincts.

5. **Will**
   Mental stability, self-control, and resistance to fear or pressure.
   > Example: staying sane, resisting terrifying scenes, fear, or pressure.

6. **Influence**
   Ability to affect others: charisma, authority, pressure.
   > Example: charm, speech, intimidation, confidence, status.

Each characteristic has a rank and a numerical modifier:

- **S** -> `+6`
- **A** -> `+4`
- **B** -> `+2`
- **C** -> `0`
- **D** -> `-2`
- **E** -> `-4`
- **F** -> `-6`

For especially strong or weak cases, extreme ranks can be repeated, such as SSS or FFF.

### Character Rank

The **Character Rank** is a general rank based on the character's characteristics. It is a loose summary of the character's overall power and does not directly affect rules.
One simple option is to choose the rank that occurs most often; if two ranks are tied, choose the higher one.

> For example, if a **Character** has characteristics A, B, B, C, C, E, they can be treated as a rank **B** character.
> These calculations can be interpreted freely and depend on the **Game Master**.

### Stats

#### Health Points (HP)

**Health Points** are a numerical description of a **Character's** vitality.
One simple way to calculate **HP** is to tie it to **Body** rank and the **Character's Health Die**. The **Health Die** can vary between characters, creatures, or archetypes. By default, use `d10`; a fragile mage might use `d6`, while a huge monster might use `d12` or higher. The base die can also be multiple dice at once, such as 2 `d8`.

Basic example for `d10`:

| Body Rank | HP | Average |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **F** | `1` | `1` |
| **E** | `5 + 1d10` | `11` |
| **D** | `15 + 2d10` | `26` |
| **C** | `30 + 3d10` | `47` |
| **B** | `50 + 4d10` | `72` |
| **A** | `90 + 5d10` | `118` |
| **S** | `150 + 6d10` | `183` |

For example, a **Character** with **Body B** and a standard **Health Die d10** gets `50 + 4d10` **HP**. If the same character is fragile and has a **Health Die d6**, they get `50 + 4d6` **HP**. As Body rank rises, HP increases more noticeably, reflecting the greater difficulty of reaching higher ranks.

This is only a basic example. If another health system fits your setting better, adapt it: fixed values, wound tracks, separate sanity, armor, or stamina tracks. The important part is that players understand what condition their **Character** is in.

#### Defense Rating (DR)

**Defense Rating** describes how physically protected a **Character** is. It can represent body toughness, dodging, fencing skill, or similar factors.

By default:

```text
DR = 10 + Body modifier + Agility modifier

```

Overall, DR can receive extra modifiers from armor, **Effects**, traits, and purely situational factors.

This is a passive defense. The **Game Master** is also encouraged to use active defense based on the situation. For example, a normal body strike against a fencer can use passive **DR**, but an attempt to hit the eye should have higher **DR**, because the target will instinctively protect it.

#### Movement Speed

Movement speed shows how far a **Character** can move during one **Turn**.

By default, all **Characters** have a speed of **5 meters**, but this can change greatly depending on the situation.

#### Exhaustion

**Exhaustion** measures how overloaded a **Character** is. The **Game Master** can use it as a universal cost for abilities and various activities, encouraging players to plan and use them efficiently. It also creates tense situations where a character is alive but too exhausted to act.

**Exhaustion** is tracked numerically. When it crosses certain thresholds, the **Character** gains penalties. The **Game Master** can allow fixed exhaustion costs or allow players to spend more or less effort. If a player tries to spend a huge amount of exhaustion to win immediately, there should be an additional cost beyond simply crossing a threshold. Treat it as pushing the body beyond its limits.

Default exhaustion states:

0. **Character** is full of strength and energy.
1. Disadvantage on **Checks**.
2. Cannot move without help and loses **Bonus Action**.
3. **Character** falls unconscious.

The threshold can vary by game, but this default formula can be used:

```text
ExhaustionThreshold_i = (5 + max(Body modifier, Mind modifier, Will modifier)) * i

```

### Death

When a **Character's HP** reaches 0, they enter the "dying" state. They are completely unable to act. If nobody helps them, they die. In **Combat**, help must arrive within 3 turns, and every attack against the dying **Character** reduces the remaining number of turns. Outside combat, the time limit is 1 minute, and any attack immediately ends the chance to help. Helping a dying character is an **Action**.

### Traits

**Traits** are features that define a **Character's** strengths and weaknesses. They should describe the character from different angles, add depth, and encourage roleplay. Traits should come from the character's story and personality.

Example traits for a soldier:

- Discipline
  Years of strict training and mental toughness. `+2` to fear checks.

- Combat weapon training, rank **A**
- Subordination
  Cannot easily disobey superiors. Will check with disadvantage to ignore orders.

- Callousness
  A harsh childhood and military life reduced their empathy. Disadvantage on charm, sympathy, and similar checks.

### Abilities

**Abilities** are special active actions a **Character** can use to strongly change a situation. They should have limits and costs so players do not abuse them. The cost should match the ability's power and the character's mastery of it.

For example, if a **Character** can fight with a halberd, normal attacks can be free. But if they invent an unusual technique, using it might cost exhaustion, and the ability rank might be low until the character masters it. Simple and common techniques may have no cost.

### Effects

**Effects** are statuses applied to a **Character**. They can be positive, negative, or neutral.

For example, if a rogue secretly puts a laxative into a **Character's** tea, that character might gain the effect "stuck in the bathroom".

#### Shells

Optional mechanic.

Many settings have some form of "shell": full armor, temporary health, a magic shield, or similar protection. These can be described as shells - extra health layers that must be broken before the character takes damage directly.

Shells can have types. In a magic setting, a shell might block only magical or only physical damage. In a psychological setting, it might be a mental shell that protects sanity.

### Items and Weapons

**Items** and **Weapons** can also have ranks. A rank can describe quality, power, rarity, convenience, or how well the **Character** has mastered the item.

Examples:

- a rusty saber might be rank **D** and give disadvantage on difficult fencing actions
- a good military rapier might be rank **B** and work reliably without penalties
- a legendary blade might be rank **A** or **S**, add bonuses, pierce special **Shells**, or unlock a separate **Ability**

An item rank does not always need to become a direct bonus. Sometimes it simply defines what the item can do. A normal knife should not easily pierce heavy armor even with a good roll, while an artifact hammer might break stone without a special formula.

### Inventory

**Inventory** is the set of **Items** a **Character** has available. The **Game Master** should allow players to carry items as long as they can explain how they store and move them. Weight can be tracked if needed, but most of the time common sense is enough.

### Inspiration

**Inspiration** is a resource the **Game Master** gives to a **Player**, usually for good play or roleplay, but ultimately at the GM's discretion.

Inspiration can be spent to:

- reroll any die
- use a **Character Ability** outside its normal rules
- give it to another **Character** for immediate use, but not for later

Optionally, the **Game Master** can use **Anti-Inspiration**. It is given for bad roleplay or acting against the character. Do not overuse it. The **Game Master** can spend it against the player who received it to:

- reroll any die
- cancel a **Character Ability**

## Conflict

A **Conflict** is a situation whose outcome depends on **Checks** from several participants. The highest result wins. On a tie, the defending side wins.

For one-sided checks, the **Game Master** sets a passive **Difficulty** before the roll.

### Difficulty

The **Game Master** sets the **Difficulty** based on the situation right before the check. It is a number. The easier and more convenient the action is, the lower the difficulty.

In opposed conflicts, characteristics usually clash directly, but the **Game Master** can add situational modifiers. Such a modifier should not exceed the maximum characteristic modifier in absolute value.

### Check

Unless stated otherwise, a **Check** uses `d20` plus the relevant characteristic modifier and any additional modifiers.

```text
1d20 + Characteristic modifier + Extra modifiers >= Difficulty

```

### Critical Success and Failure

A critical success or failure does not necessarily lead to automatic success or a deadly outcome. By default, a natural 20 is a critical success and a natural 1 is a critical failure. **Traits** and **Abilities** can change these values.

Absolutes do not belong here: consequences should make the result stand out without allowing absurd outcomes. Think "a small miracle" or "really bad luck."

### Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantage** means the **Character** rolls twice and takes the better result. **Disadvantage** means they take the worse result.

A critical success overrides any disadvantage. A critical failure overrides any advantage.

Advantages and disadvantages usually should not stack. Pick the overall state and limit the roll to 2 dice unless a specific **Trait** or **Ability** says otherwise.

## Combat

**Combat** is split into **Rounds**, and each round contains **Turns** for participating **Characters**.

### Initiative

Before **Combat**, all participants roll initiative using **Agility**. This determines turn order. On a tie, tied characters reroll between themselves, or act in an order agreed by the players or decided by the **Game Master**.

### Turns

Combat is divided into **Turns**, each belonging to one **Character**. Characters can delay their turn to the end of the round. Allied **Characters** whose turns are consecutive can act together.

Each **Turn** lasts roughly 6 seconds in the game world.

If players think for too long, the GM can push them narratively or use a real time limit. A good option is "hesitation": if a player cannot decide for too long, the character hesitates and loses the turn. This should also apply to the **Game Master**.

### Zones

Combat space is divided into zones. A **Character** can move freely within their current zone. Moving beyond it costs movement.

Usually, a field can be divided into 1-meter cells and characters have 5 meters of movement, meaning 5 cells per turn. This is only an example. Movement systems can change with the setting and scene.

### Actions

By default, a **Character** can take 1 **Action** and 1 **Bonus Action** per turn, not counting movement. **Traits** and **Abilities** can change this.

Actions must fit within the turn and be possible in the situation. Example actions:

1. **Attack**
   Damage, push, wound, or otherwise harm an enemy.

2. **Study / Analyze**
   Gain useful information about space, characters, items, and so on.

3. **Influence**
   Affect another **Character's** behavior without a physical attack.

4. **Search**
   Find hidden things.

5. **Resist**
   Try to remove an **Effect** or resist its influence.

6. **Prepare**
   Prepare a larger action for the next turn. This can also grant **Advantage**, such as aiming.

7. **Team Action**
   Combine your **Turn** with another **Character's Turn**. Enemy turns must not separate the two turns.

8. **Interact**
   Any action not listed above.

If an action can be done in a brief moment, it can be done as a **Bonus Action**.

### Reactions

On another character's turn, a **Character** can only make **Reactions**: defend against area attacks, resist **Effects**, or respond to interaction with them or their immediate surroundings.

A **Reaction** must fit the same limits as a **Bonus Action**. By default, a **Character** can make 1 **Reaction** per **Round**. The **Game Master** decides what can be done as a reaction, while players can argue why a reaction should be possible.

## Rest

During a game or campaign, **Players** need rests. **Short Rests** are usually started by players, while **Long Rests** usually happen between sessions.

### Short Rest

During a **Short Rest**, **Resources** recover partially depending on the time spent resting, up to half of their maximum values.

### Long Rest

During a **Long Rest**, all **Resources** recover, and all **Effects** and **Exhaustion** are cleared unless stated otherwise.
Long rests usually happen automatically between sessions.

# For Players

## Character Creation

A player starts by creating their **Character**. They should decide who they want to be in the adventure and what they can do. This should be discussed with the **Game Master** so the character fits the game.

### Concept

Start with the character's concept: their rough goals, wishes, and capabilities. They should fit the setting provided by the **Game Master**.

### Backstory

After the concept is approved, write the character's backstory so their idea has narrative grounding. The level of detail depends on your table. You can write only a sketch and let the **Game Master** fill gaps, or write more yourself and let the GM adjust it.

### Mechanics

Based on the concept and the game systems, the player and GM define the character's mechanics:

- Characteristics
- Traits
- Abilities

### Equipment

Most characters do not appear empty-handed. Decide what inventory the character has at the start. As with backstory, this can be handled by the player, the **Game Master**, or both.

## Progression

**Character progression** happens in several ways:

- Changing **Characteristics**
  **Characters** can improve in some areas over time. If they neglect an area, characteristics can also decrease.

- Increasing survivability
  After serious trials, the **Game Master** can reward **Characters** with more **HP**, a better **Health Die**, a defensive **Trait**, or a stronger **Shell**.

- **Traits** and **Abilities**
  During adventures, **Characters** can change, transform, learn new things, gain new traits and abilities, or increase their ranks.

- New **Items**
  New **Items** are also progression. The **Game Master** should give players chances to obtain useful items, unique artifacts, and sometimes even legendary things.

## Roleplay

Roleplay is one of the most important parts of the game. Even combat-heavy games benefit from it. It adds drama, depth, immersion, and fun. Any interaction, conflict, or short exchange can become more engaging when roleplayed.

Simple ways to make roleplay easier:

- Think about the character's voice and how it changes in different situations.
- Think about facial expressions and reactions to sadness, joy, irritation, and so on.
- Feel the character's story. If it is short, add small details and imagine more.

# For Game Masters

## Supporting Players

Throughout preparation, give your **Players** all the support and help they need so they feel comfortable and are not overwhelmed by lore or mechanics. Make their lives easier, but do not limit what they want. Even if they want divine powers, let them have a small piece of that potential and a seed that can grow into the power they want. Different people care about different parts of play, so always look for an individual approach and create a comfortable environment around the game itself. Inside the game, the rules are different...

The same support should continue during the campaign, between sessions: discuss what players like and dislike, talk about things that interest them, and imagine their characters' futures with them.

## Story

The first thing you will probably begin preparing for a campaign is its story and characters. Planning and preparing a game can be a long and laborious process, but it is worth it. Before you start, think through the setting, plots, and characters. Think through how they intertwine and possible plot turns, then write and structure it all so it is easier to return to later. You do not have to invent everything from scratch: adapt existing stories, worlds, or characters.

It is also strongly recommended to think about ***what*** story you want to tell and how it can end. Do not leave planning the ending until the last moment. Even a rough outline of the story should exist before play begins. At the same time, do not try to write every detail immediately: there is a good chance it will all go in the bin once the players take a path you did not expect.

### Setting

Start by describing your setting. Even when adapting an existing one, you need to describe its rules and nuances in a structured format that works for you. There are plenty of good resources about this online, so there is little point in rambling about it here.

### Plotline

Now choose a plotline that develops in the setting you wrote. It does not have to be the party's plot. In fact, it is better to describe events that are happening in the world: war, the end of the world, a treasure hunt, rescuing a princess. Describe what has happened in the world to understand the situation your **Characters** find themselves in.

### Major Figures

Once you have written the setting and plotline, also write down the major characters and their motivations. These can be not only non-player **Characters**, but player **Characters** too. Knowing their motivations and capabilities in advance makes it much easier to run the story and improvise during sessions.

### Locations

Once you know most of what the story needs, describing and planning locations remains important. These are where plot events can take place and where **Players** can end up. Knowing locations and their connections to the plot lets you add hints on the fly and makes session preparation much easier.

### Length

Last but not least is understanding the story's length. You may not know the exact number, but it is worth estimating how many sessions you have before you or the players get tired, and how many sessions you need at minimum to finish the story. This helps you understand what scale is available or needed. Limits can be irritating, but they are useful and can even make creativity better.

## Scenes

Variations and sequences of game events needed to move an adventure to its next stage.

### Types

1. Negotiation
   Events about relationships between **Characters** and exchanging information.

2. Exploration
   Events about looking for resources and information while studying the game **Space**.

3. Combat
   Events about opposition between **Characters**, with risk to their **Health**.

4. Travel
   Events about movement and overcoming obstacles within the game **Space**.

5. Stealth
   Events about moving through the game **Space** while avoiding detection.

6. Interlude
   Events in which a **Player** has limited participation or acts as a spectator.

7. Sandbox
   Events about free interaction with the game **Space**, **Characters**, and **Resources**.

### Scene Building Rules

Every scene prepared by the **Game Master** should have the following elements. Scenes that Players get themselves into do not count:

- **Description**
  Every scene needs a description. You do not have to describe every millimeter of space, but you should describe the distinctive features of the current situation. The stale, heavy smell in a room with a corpse or the sweet, nose-hitting smell of drinks in a tavern are vivid details that immerse players and set the mood at the start of a scene.

- **Sound**
  Every scene has its own sound. Ideally, it changes from scene to scene and sometimes during a scene, accompanying and supporting the story.

- **Emotional shift**
  Like in film, if emotions do not change during a scene, it only stretches time instead of adding enjoyment. This does not mean every scene needs drama or a plot twist. Strong shocks should alternate with weaker ones to keep the game alive. Add light heart-to-heart conversations between heavy plot turns, and let players relax and have fun between saving, or destroying, the world.

- **Heroic moment**
  In every scene, at least one player should have a chance to shine. It is difficult to create situations where everyone in the party can shine, so give each **Character** an opportunity every so often. Also, sometimes give several characters, or ideally the whole party, a chance to show teamwork.

- **Change**
  There should always be an opportunity to change something in the scene, either through player actions or events they know about, such as a timer. A scene should be able to gain momentum, move forward, and move the story with it.

- **Visual support**
  *Optional, but strongly recommended.*
  You cannot find a perfect image for every scene, but you can at least find a distant or abstract image that players can latch onto and build the rest from your description.

## Improvisation

Players will probably behave differently from what you expected, so you will find yourself in situations you did not plan. In these cases, the best advice is not to "fight the current," but to let it guide you. Treat unusual **Player** actions as new opportunities. There is no need to constantly pull them back onto your plot rails: you can always return them there, just by a longer route.

Still, do not hesitate to give players a telling-off, in game of course, if they start doing nothing but completely nonsensical things.

## Balance and Damage

Balancing combat and damage is difficult, especially in a flexible system. The **Game Master** can freely adapt weapons, armor, damage, health, and enemy features from other TTRPGs if that makes preparation faster. There is no need to build a new mathematical model every time.

The main idea stays the same: simplicity matters more than a strict formula. Damage is not only for subtracting numbers from **HP**, but also for showing how well a **Character** handled a situation. A good attack can deal more damage, break protection, disarm an enemy, knock them down, or create an advantage for an ally.

Damage can be based on ranks:

- weak or unsuitable weapon: `1d6` or less
- ordinary combat weapon: `1d10`
- good weapon or a favorable situation: `2d10`
- strong ability, heavy weapon, or a clear advantage: `3d10` or more

For example, when a **Character** with a rapier simply hits an enemy, it can deal `1d10` damage. If they distracted the enemy in advance, attacked a vulnerable point, and passed the check by a large margin, the **Game Master** can raise the damage to `2d10`, add bleeding, or even allow an instant kill against a weak enemy.

The **Game Master** should encourage players to resolve combat creatively: use the environment, talk, set traps, retreat, split enemies, break equipment, or change the conflict's goal. If combat becomes only an exchange of blows, damage still works, but the system shows less of what it can do.

## Character Deaths

This is a fairly individual subject, but one piece of advice still applies: do not make characters unable to die. Every character should be able to perish in at least some sense; otherwise, life has less value to Players. This applies to player characters and non-player characters alike.

## Before The Game

Before a session, it is strongly recommended to have short interviews with each player about their character and the world. You can even play out short narrative scenes. This helps **Players** feel and understand their **Characters**, and helps you understand how you will interact with them.

## Tools

These tools can help with game preparation and running sessions:

- [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md/) - notes, campaign organization, databases, and session preparation.
- [Watabou's Procgen Arcana](https://watabou.github.io/) - world, town, dungeon, and location generators.
- [Donjon](https://donjon.bin.sh/) - generators for names, encounters, treasure, maps, and other preparation material.
- [Tabletop Audio](https://tabletopaudio.com/) - background music and sound pads.
- [Roll20](https://roll20.net/) - virtual tabletop for maps, tokens, dice, sheets, and remote play.

# Flow of Play

Each game should start with the **Game Master**. The GM gives an interlude and brings **Players** into the situation. If the game has multiple sessions, this can include a recap. A good option is to ask one **Player** to summarize what happened last time.

Then **Players** enter the opening **Scene**, and play follows a cycle:

- **Players** take actions
- the world reacts
- **Players** move to the next **Scene**
- repeat until they reach a final **Scene**

The **Game Master** should prepare consequences for **Player** actions and ways to return the game to tension if a scene starts falling apart or losing pace. Do not force **Players** down one required path. It is better to understand what conflicts, threats, and opportunities can grow from their decisions.

At the end of the game, the **Game Master** should say final words that summarize the adventure and possible future events, so **Players** keep thinking about the game after leaving.
